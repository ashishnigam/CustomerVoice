# CustomerVoice Brand Guidelines

## 1. Purpose
This document defines the CustomerVoice brand system for product, marketing, design, engineering, and AI agents. Use it to keep future screens, pages, and visual assets coherent.

## 2. Brand position
CustomerVoice is not a generic idea box. It is a feedback operations platform.

The product promise:
1. Give customers a clear public place to request and vote on features.
2. Give product teams an internal operating layer for moderation, prioritization, and outreach.
3. Keep product delivery visible after work ships.

## 3. Brand personality
1. Clear, practical, and trustworthy.
2. Strategic rather than playful.
3. Operationally serious without looking cold or bureaucratic.
4. Founder-credible and enterprise-aware.

## 4. Audience cues
Primary audiences:
1. Founders and product leaders at early and growth-stage SaaS companies.
2. Product, engineering, and operations teams that need a structured public feedback process.
3. Enterprise-leaning buyers who care about security, deployment posture, and roadmap visibility.

## 5. Voice and copy rules
Do:
1. Write in direct business language.
2. Emphasize outcomes, operating leverage, and workflow clarity.
3. Be precise about what is implemented versus what is roadmap.
4. Use verbs like `capture`, `moderate`, `prioritize`, `notify`, `close the loop`.

Do not:
1. Use vague hype such as `revolutionize`, `game-changing`, or `AI-powered everything`.
2. Claim compliance or enterprise readiness beyond what is actually true.
3. Sound like a lightweight community forum product when describing the platform.

## 6. Visual direction
The visual language should feel:
1. Bright and structured.
2. Layered and dimensional rather than flat.
3. Anchored by deep blue trust tones.
4. Supported by green signals for progress and completion.
5. Editorial in headlines and product-focused in body copy.

## 7. Color system
Core brand colors:
1. `#143F64` deep trust blue.
2. `#2F7BB1` bright product blue.
3. `#5FB48E` progress green.
4. `#F5FAFF` light canvas.
5. `#EEF6FF` panel background.
6. `#10253D` body text.
7. `#597592` secondary text.

Usage rules:
1. Use deep blue for anchors, primary actions, and emphasis.
2. Use green as a supporting accent, not the dominant brand color.
3. Keep backgrounds light and layered; avoid flat white-only layouts.
4. Use gentle gradients and soft border treatments to create depth.

## 8. Typography
1. Headlines: expressive serif, currently `Iowan Old Style` family fallback stack.
2. UI and body: clean sans-serif, currently `Sora` with practical fallbacks.
3. Headlines should be compact, high-contrast, and slightly editorial.
4. Body copy should remain concise and highly readable.

## 9. Layout rules
Marketing pages:
1. Lead with one strong hero and one clear product thesis.
2. Pair copy with illustrations or structured visual artifacts.
3. Use sections that alternate between proof, workflow, and buyer use cases.
4. Avoid long walls of undifferentiated cards.

Product pages:
1. Separate setup/configuration surfaces from customer-facing preview surfaces.
2. Make the board route visible and easy to copy.
3. Prioritize idea discovery, status clarity, and voting actions.
4. Internal controls should be present but not overwhelm the board preview.

## 10. Illustration system
1. Prefer custom SVG or geometric product illustrations over stock photos.
2. Illustrations should represent workflows, board surfaces, or enterprise posture.
3. Use rounded frames, light gradients, and structured blocks that resemble product surfaces.
4. Keep illustrations light-themed and compatible with the broader brand palette.

## 11. Motion
1. Motion should be minimal and purposeful.
2. Favor subtle reveal, hover, or state transitions.
3. Avoid noisy micro-interactions or animated decoration with no product meaning.

## 12. Product experience rules
1. A board is a product object, not just a list selection.
2. Every board should have a canonical route that can be copied and bookmarked.
3. Board configuration should feel secondary to the customer-facing board experience.
4. Moderation and analytics should feel like internal operating tools layered behind the public board.

## 13. Marketing experience rules
1. The homepage should sell the category and the product thesis, not local setup details.
2. Developer docs belong in docs routes, not in the hero narrative.
3. Pricing should remain honest about beta status and planned packaging.
4. Blog content should support SEO, market education, and investor narrative.

## 14. Accessibility rules
1. Keep strong contrast for text and actions.
2. Do not communicate state through color alone.
3. Maintain clear focus order and predictable interaction patterns.
4. Ensure layouts degrade cleanly on tablet and mobile.

## 15. Implementation guardrails for AI agents
1. Check this file before creating new marketing or product surfaces.
2. Keep the distinction clear between public board experience and internal operating surfaces.
3. Reuse the core color, typography, and illustration logic before inventing new styles.
4. If new roadmap claims are added to marketing pages, verify they match the live execution documents.
